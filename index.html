How Long This Lasts

Term, renewal, and when either party can end this agreement

In Simple Terms

This Agreement starts on the Effective Date and runs for 36 months (three years). After that, it automatically renews unless one of you gives the other at least 90 days' notice that you don't want to renew. If rates change, we'll tell you at least 60 days in advance. If you want to leave, you have a 30-day notice period before the end of each year—after that, we move forward together for another full year.

Auto-Renewal: At the end of the Initial Term and at the end of each renewal term thereafter, this Agreement automatically renews for successive 36-month periods on the same terms and conditions, unless either party provides written notice of non-renewal at least ninety (90) days before the end of the then-current term. What "Response Time" Means

Response time = time to acknowledge and start working, NOT time to fix. When you submit a ticket, our response SLA clock starts ticking. We commit to having a Trisian engineer acknowledge your ticket and begin diagnostics within the timeframe above. Resolution time depends on complexity — it could take longer to actually fix the issue, depending on root cause and what needs to be done. We don't promise fixed issues in the same timeframe as our response.

How Priority Levels Are Assigned

When you submit a ticket through our portal, our automated ticketing system (HaloPSA) classifies it based on a grid of Impact × Urgency:

  • Impact: How many users are affected? (All users = Critical; Department = High; Individual = Medium/Low)
  • Urgency: How time-sensitive is it? (Prevents work = Critical; Degrades work = High; Nice to fix = Low)

Our system auto-classifies based on your submission, but Trisian reserves the right to reclassify. If you report something as P1 "Critical" but our investigation shows the actual impact is low or your users have a workaround, we may downgrade the priority. Likewise, if a P4 ticket reveals a more serious underlying issue, we'll upgrade it.
